# Layer 1 research notes: filing-language drift as a tradeable signal

The defensible Layer 1 design is **lexical-first, language-feature-augmented, and embedding-secondary** — and the evidence does not support naming this layer "Embedding Drift" if name has to follow signal. Cohen, Malloy and Nguyen (2020, *Journal of Finance*) extracted the entire Lazy Prices anomaly using bag-of-words cosine, Jaccard, normalized Levenshtein, and a "simple" similarity ratio — none of them embeddings. Tang & Yang's 2025 FinMTEB benchmark (EMNLP 2025 Main) finds, surprisingly, that **a simple bag-of-words approach outperforms sophisticated dense embeddings on financial Semantic Textual Similarity tasks**, and Liu-Yang-Tam's 2024 NAACL paper "Beyond Surface Similarity" demonstrates that off-the-shelf transformer embeddings (including OpenAI text-embedding-3-large) fail to detect the subtle period-over-period semantic shifts that this layer is built to capture. The frontier 2024–2026 literature has *not* superseded TF-IDF cosine for the change-detection use case; it has refined where dense methods do and do not earn their cost.

That sets the priors. The 188 bps/month figure that everyone quotes from Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen is in fact the **5-factor, section-specific (Risk Factors), 1995–2014 in-sample** estimate; the full-document value-weighted alpha is closer to 34–58 bps/month with t ≈ 3.59. There is **no peer-reviewed academic replication of Lazy Prices on 2014–2024 out-of-sample data** as of April 2026 — itself a defensible interview observation, and an obvious internal experiment for this layer to run. Practitioner replications (Alpha Architect; community GitHub at calebyung/NLP-10-K-10-Q-Alpha-Research) report Sharpe ratios near 1.5 with materially compressed magnitudes, consistent with the McLean-Pontiff (2016) post-publication anomaly-decay framework that estimates ~58% mean attenuation across 97 anomalies. Bayesian-shrunk expectation for a current realistic FF5 alpha target: **3–8% annualized gross**, ~100 bps after frictions at $1B notional.

The remainder of this memo documents the literature, the financial mechanism, the data infrastructure, the three methodological families, validation strategy, failure modes, open questions, and the cross-layer schema. The score schema and provenance are designed to support clean joins to Layer 2 (Insider Divergence) and Layer 4 (Public Discourse) on `(cik, ticker, filing_date, section)` from the first commit.

## 1. Foundational and frontier literature

### Canonical pre-2022 foundation

**Cohen, Malloy & Nguyen (2020), "Lazy Prices," *Journal of Finance* 75(3): 1371–1415.** Sample is the universe of 10-K, 10-K405, 10-KSB and 10-Q filings on EDGAR for 1995–2014 — order of 140,000 filings — with no size or liquidity filtering. Methodology is four parallel similarity measures over the union vocabulary of consecutive same-type filings: cosine on raw term-frequency vectors, Jaccard on token sets, normalized minimum-edit (Levenshtein) distance, and a "simple" word-track-changes ratio. Tables, exhibits and HTML-embedded numerics are stripped using Bill McDonald's SRAF protocol; per-section measures are computed after Item-header regex parsing. Portfolios sort firms each month into quintiles of similarity, hold 3 months post-filing, rebalance monthly with one-month lag.

The headline value-weighted long-non-changers/short-changers Jaccard portfolio earns a **34–58 bps/month four-factor alpha (t = 3.59)** at the full-document level. The often-cited **188 bps/month** is the five-factor alpha on the **Risk Factors section specifically (t = 2.76)**, the strongest section-specific result. Litigation-keyword-restricted variants earn 71 bps/month (t = 3.29). Critically, the announcement-window CAR is statistically and economically zero — the alpha accrues only with weeks-to-months lag, in stark contrast to PEAD-style drift. About 86% of textual changes carry negative-sentiment loadings (the 14% positive ones predict positive future returns), an asymmetry the authors attribute to litigation-risk-driven downside disclosure. The effect is not concentrated in microcaps (changers average $3.5B vs $2.5B market cap for non-changers), short fees on changers are if anything below average, and limits-to-arbitrage explanations are explicitly ruled out. Length and Δlength are explicit Fama-MacBeth controls, and the alpha survives. Inattention is identified by SEC EDGAR FOIA log files: when investors concurrently download both this year's and last year's 10-K (proxy `IPAccessMultipleYear`), predictability attenuates significantly — direct support for inattention rather than risk premium.

**Loughran & McDonald (2011), "When Is a Liability Not a Liability?" *Journal of Finance* 66(1): 35–65.** Sample ~50,115 10-K firm-years, 1994–2008. Six finance-specific word lists derived from words appearing in ≥5% of all 10-Ks: **Negative (2,329 words), Positive (354), Uncertainty, Litigious, Strong-Modal, Weak-Modal**; Bodnaruk-Loughran-McDonald (2015) added Constraining (184 words); LM 2024 added Complexity. The paper's signature critique: nearly three-quarters of the words flagged as negative by the Harvard General Inquirer are not negative in financial contexts (*liability, tax, capital, vice, board*). LM-negative is significantly negatively associated with filing-period [0,3] abnormal returns at t-statistics consistently larger than Harvard's, positively associated with trading volume, post-filing volatility, subsequent material-weakness disclosures, AAER fraud classifications, and negative unexpected earnings. TF-IDF weighting (their Eq. 1) roughly doubles the t-statistic vs equally weighted counts. The 2020 Master Dictionary update *removed* tokens like BREAKING, BRIDGE, BROKEN, CLOSED, CRITICAL, FORCE, LATE from Negative, and BENEFICIAL, BENEFIT, EFFECTIVE, GREAT, HONORABLE from Positive — meaning sentiment scores are not version-stable and the 2024 release with Complexity must be pinned by SHA.

**Brown & Tucker (2011), "Large-Sample Evidence on Firms' Year-Over-Year MD&A Modifications," *Journal of Accounting Research* 49(2): 309–346.** ~24,000 MD&A pairs 1997–2006. **The direct technical precursor to Lazy Prices:** vector-space-model cosine with TF-IDF on consecutive-year MD&A. They prove analytically (Appendix B) that **cosine similarity converges mechanically to 1 as document length grows** because shared common-word vocabulary swamps document-specific tokens. Their fix — regress the raw 1−cosine score on log(document length) and use the residual — is the canonical correction. Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen do *not* uniformly apply this orthogonalization, which is a methodological criticism flagged by replicators and a major reason length-normalization must be central in any 2026 implementation.

**Loughran & McDonald (2014), "Measuring Readability in Financial Disclosures," *Journal of Finance* 69(4): 1643–1671.** Demolishes the Fog Index for finance: complex words (≥3 syllables) include trivially-understood tokens like *company, financial, telecommunications*. They recommend **log(10-K file size)** as a simpler, harder-to-game proxy. Bonsall, Leone, Miller and Rennekamp (2017, *JAE*) introduce the Bog Index from SEC plain-English software but this is proprietary.

**Li (2008, 2010).** "Annual Report Readability, Current Earnings, and Earnings Persistence" (*JAE* 45(2-3): 221–247) launched the readability literature on ~55,719 10-Ks 1994–2004; firms with lower current earnings have higher Fog (β ≈ 0.15, t ≈ 8.4) and earnings persistence is materially lower for high-Fog firms. "The Information Content of Forward-Looking Statements" (*JAR* 48(5): 1049–1102) hand-codes ~30,000 MD&A sentences and trains a Naïve Bayes classifier across 140,000 firm-quarters; FLS-positive minus FLS-negative ratios predict next-quarter ROA and mitigate accruals mispricing. Methodologically superseded by transformer classifiers, but the FLS taxonomy remains the academic standard.

**Hoberg & Lewis (2017), "Do Fraudulent Firms Produce Abnormal Disclosure?" *Journal of Corporate Finance* 43: 58–85.** ~68,000 MD&A firm-years 1997–2010. Computes cosine similarity of each firm-year's MD&A vs its **Industry × Size × Age peer cohort** — *abnormal disclosure = deviation from peers*. Latent Dirichlet Allocation extracts 75 topics; 12 are significantly associated with AAER years; fraudulent firms abnormally *under*-disclose performance-driver topics. Augmented F-score raises fraud detection by ~25% over Dechow-Ge-Larson-Sloan (2011) baseline. The peer-distance cosine paradigm is foundational for any anomaly-detection layer.

**Hoberg & Phillips (2010, 2016).** "Text-Based Network Industries and Endogenous Product Differentiation" *JPE* 124(5): 1423–1465. Item 1 Business Description text from all 10-Ks; pairwise cosine similarity each year-firm; TNIC industries built by setting threshold to match SIC pair-count. The benchmark proof that **cosine on 10-K text outperforms SIC/NAICS** for explaining profitability, Tobin's Q, M&A success and asset-pricing covariation. **Their 2025 *JoF* update ("Scope, Scale, and Concentration: The 21st-Century Firm," 80(1): 415–466) replaced TF-IDF cosine with Doc2Vec PV-DBOW** — an explicit acknowledgment by the original cosine-on-10-K authors that distributed representations now justify the cost on at least some classification tasks. Public data at hobergphillips.tuck.dartmouth.edu.

**Campbell, Chen, Dhaliwal, Lu & Steele (2014), "The Information Content of Mandatory Risk Factor Disclosures," *RAS* 19(1): 396–455.** ~9,000 10-Ks 2005–2008 (Item 1A mandate began 2005). Shows risk-factor text is firm-specific, not boilerplate, and disclosure intensity correlates with post-filing systematic and idiosyncratic risk, information asymmetry, and Tobin's Q. Provides the theoretical basis for why Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen find the strongest section-specific alpha in Risk Factors.

**Bodnaruk, Loughran & McDonald (2015), "Using 10-K Text to Gauge Financial Constraints," *JFQA* 50(4): 623–646.** 184-word constraining-words list (*required, obligated, impair, covenants, mandatory*) — added as the seventh sentiment list. Correlation with KZ, WW, SA, HP indices is only 0.1–0.2 — text captures distinct information. Predicts dividend omissions and underfunded pensions out-of-sample at t > 5.

**You & Zhang (2009), "Financial Reporting Complexity and Investor Underreaction to 10-K Information," *RAS* 14(4): 559–586.** ~37,287 10-Ks 1995–2005. Significant filing-window CAR (~0.3% over [-1,+1]) plus 12-month post-filing drift in the direction of the initial reaction; drift is stronger for higher-complexity firms (top decile drift ≈ 2× bottom decile). Direct intellectual ancestor of Lazy Prices.

**Dyer, Lang & Stice-Lawrence (2017), *JAE* 64(2-3): 221–245.** Sample 75,991 firm-years 1996–2013. **Median 10-K length doubled from 23,000 words (1996) to ~50,000 (2013)**; three topics (fair value/impairment, internal controls, risk factors) account for nearly all the increase. Boilerplate, redundancy and stickiness all rise nearly monotonically. This is the critical length-bias paper for any 2020s implementation: as 10-Ks have grown to 80,000+ words, the same Jaccard threshold partitions a fundamentally different cross-section than in 2000.

### Modern era 2022–2026 — what has actually moved

**The single most important 2024 paper for this layer's design is Liu, Yang & Tam (2024), "Beyond Surface Similarity: Detecting Subtle Semantic Shifts in Financial Narratives," NAACL Findings, arXiv 2403.14341.** It introduces the **Financial-STS task** for detecting subtle year-over-year semantic shifts in same-firm 10-Ks. Off-the-shelf models — Sentence-BERT, OpenAI text-embedding-3-large, generic LLM embeddings — *fail* to discriminate the subtle shifts that drive the Lazy Prices effect. Their LLM-augmented contrastive pipeline outperforms generic STS-trained embeddings on a human-annotated FinSTS dataset. The implication for this layer is direct: **Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen's bag-of-words/cosine/Jaccard pipeline may be competitive with naïve embedding methods in 2026** unless the embedding is finance-fine-tuned and trained explicitly for subtle semantic shift.

**Tang et al. (2025), "FinMTEB: Finance Massive Text Embedding Benchmark," EMNLP 2025 Main.** FinBERT beats general BERT by +15.6% on FinMTEB averages (0.6721 vs 0.5812); their **Fin-E5 (e5-mistral-7b-instruct fine-tuned on financial tasks) reaches 0.6767**, exceeding OpenAI text-embedding-3-large and Voyage-3-large. Strongest gains in classification (0.842) and STS (0.721). The decisive empirical finding: domain-adapted models consistently outperform general-purpose, *and a simple bag-of-words approach outperforms sophisticated dense embeddings on financial STS*. As of April 2026 Fin-E5 is the state-of-the-art among publicly available finance embedding models.

**Jehnen, Villalba-Díez & Ordieres-Meré (2025), "FinTextSim," arXiv 2504.15683 / *Frontiers in AI* 2026.** ModernBERT fine-tuned on financial text with adaptive circle loss applied to S&P 500 Item 7/7A 10-K text 2016–2023. Raises intratopic similarity by 81% and reduces intertopic similarity by 100% relative to all-MiniLM-L6-v2. BERTopic+FinTextSim produces clean topic clusters where BERTopic+MiniLM produces overlapping noise. Most rigorous BERTopic-on-10K paper to date.

**Kim, Muhn & Nikolaev (2023, multiple revisions), "Bloated Disclosures: Can ChatGPT Help Investors Process Information?" arXiv 2306.10224.** Their **v5 was withdrawn 9 October 2025** — flag this; the authors are revising. Working result: GPT-3.5 summaries are ~70% shorter than originals yet contain amplified sentiment that better explains announcement-window returns; "bloat" (original-vs-summary length ratio) is associated with lower price efficiency and higher information asymmetry. Treat as working evidence, *not* peer-reviewed.

**Cao, Jiang, Yang & Zhang (2023), "How to Talk When a Machine Is Listening," *Review of Financial Studies* 36(9): 3603–3642.** **The single most important caveat for this layer's design.** Documents that growing AI/machine readership of EDGAR causally motivates firms to adjust filing language to be friendlier to machine processing: firms reduce LM-negative dictionary words; tones rated negative by computational algorithms decline relative to tones rated negative only by human-readable dictionaries; high-machine-readership firms show speech in conference calls assessed as more positive by audio processors. Loughran-McDonald (2011) and BERT (2018) publication serve as instrumental events identifying machine-readership effects. **Filing language has been partially endogenous to algorithmic readers since at least 2011, increasingly so post-2018, and explicitly more so post-2022.** Any signal we extract is endogenous to its own publication. Defenses: prefer structural drift signals (length, section reorganization, modal-verb patterns) harder to cosmetically game than dictionary-sentiment signals; expect signal decay post-2018 in any backtest.

**Other modern era anchors worth citing in the doc:**
- **Huang, Wang & Yang (2023), *Contemporary Accounting Research* 40(2): 806–841 — FinBERT.** 88.2% out-of-sample sentence-level accuracy on analyst-report sentiment vs LM dictionary 62.1%, NB 73.6%, SVM 72.6%, generic BERT 85.0%. FinBERT subsumes LM in horse-race regressions on returns *for sentiment classification* — but this is a different task than period-over-period drift.
- **PEAD.txt (Meursault et al., *JFQA* 58(6): 2299–2326, 2023).** 138K earnings press releases; FinBERT-text-only PEAD = 8.01%, text+number = 6.15%. Confirms FinBERT's value in earnings-text drift specifically.
- **Sautner, van Lent, Vilkov & Zhang (2023), *JoF* 78(3): 1449–1498.** Earnings-call bigram-based climate exposure; foundation for climate-text papers.
- **Florackis, Louca, Michaely & Weber (2023), *RFS* 36(1): 351–407.** Cyber-RF text predicts cyber-risk premium of up to 8.3%/year; structurally identical methodology (similarity to a labeled-event training corpus, applied within Item 1A) to what we deploy.
- **Sentinel survey: arXiv 2504.07274 (2025) "Language Modeling for the Future of Finance"** reviews 374 NLP papers 2017–2024.

### What is contested or unsettled

The **post-2014 Lazy Prices alpha** has not been formally re-tested in a peer-reviewed sample. The **magnitude of the original alpha** is widely misquoted: 188 bps/month is section-specific (Risk Factors), 5-factor, in-sample; the central full-document estimate is 34–58 bps/month with t ≈ 3.59. Independent practitioner replications find ~50–70% shrinkage. Whether **transformer embeddings empirically beat lexical methods on the drift-prediction task** is contested: FinBERT/Fin-E5/FinTextSim materially beat LM on labeled classification (10–35% F1), but their advantage on the *change-detection* task is unproven and likely much smaller; FinMTEB explicitly shows BoW beats dense on financial STS. The **Fog Index is broken** for finance (Loughran-McDonald 2014); use log(file size) instead. **DEF 14A is genuinely under-explored** in modern literature relative to 10-Ks — a Layer 1 opportunity.

## 2. Quant-finance grounding — why drift predicts returns

### Why stable language predicts outperformance

The Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen mechanism rests on four pillars. **Managerial entrenchment**: stable filings reflect stable strategy, stable operations, stable governance — and the empirical record shows entrenched-but-stable firms outperform reorganizing-but-unstable ones over 3–18 month horizons (consistent with Brown-Tucker's evidence that LCR-driven MD&A modifications are stronger predictors than ΔEPS-driven ones). **Change-concealment**: managers hide bad news in language modifications, betting investors won't notice — supported by the asymmetry (86% of changes carry negative sentiment loadings) and by Florackis et al.'s finding that the cyber-language signal is concentrated in firms that under-disclosed and were later hacked. **Investor inattention**: filings are too long for retail investors and even institutional analysts miss section-level changes. Loughran-McDonald (2017, *Journal of Behavioral Finance* 18: 231–248) find the average 10-K is downloaded only ~28 times by humans in the days post-filing. **Market under-reaction with lag**: Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen's announcement-window CAR is zero; the alpha accrues only over weeks-to-months — the signature of slow information diffusion (You-Zhang 2009; Tetlock 2007; Engelberg's costly-information-processing framework).

### Why drift could also predict returns (the bullish-drift case)

Genuine strategic transformation, restructuring, or new disclosure mandates can drive drift. New CEOs and CFOs frequently rewrite MD&A in their own voice within 12 months of arrival. Drift is therefore directionally ambiguous *a priori*; the literature finds the negative-drift case dominates on average (CMN 86% asymmetry) but with a non-trivial 14% positively-loaded tail. The asymmetry varies with company type: small-cap drift more often signals concealment or distress; large-cap drift more often reflects legitimate change because megacap operations turn over slowly. An important corollary: the *direction* of drift (toward more uncertain, more negative, more litigious language) is strictly more useful than magnitude alone — which is why specific-language-feature drift earns its place alongside the agnostic similarity scores.

### What filings actually are

The **10-K** structure (current as of FY2024+) is Items 1, 1A Risk Factors, 1B, **1C Cybersecurity (new for FY2023+)**, 2, 3 Legal, 4 Mine Safety, 5, **6 [Reserved] (formerly Selected Financial Data, eliminated Feb 2021)**, 7 MD&A, 7A Quant/Qual Market Risk (not required for SRCs), 8 Financial Statements, 9, 9A Controls, 9B, **9C HFCAA Foreign Jurisdictions (since 2021)**, 10–14 (governance, comp, ownership, related-party, accountant fees — typically incorporated by reference from DEF 14A), 15 Exhibits. The **10-Q** is Part I (condensed financials, MD&A, market risk, controls) and Part II (legal, risk-factor updates, equity, defaults, mine safety, other, exhibits — Rule 10b5-1 trading-plan disclosures appear in Part II Item 5 since 2023). The **20-F** has 19 numbered items grouped in Parts I–IV, with Item 3.D Risk Factors, Item 5 Operating and Financial Review (FPI MD&A equivalent), Item 11 Quant/Qual Market Risk, **Item 16K Cybersecurity (new FY2023+)**, Item 18 Financial Statements; FPIs are typically 200–500 pages, much longer than 10-Ks. The **8-K** has 22 triggering items 1.01–9.01 since the August 2004 modernization; the must-cover items for finance applications are 1.01 (material agreement), **1.05 (cybersecurity, new Dec 2023)**, 2.01 (acquisition), 2.02 (earnings), 2.05/2.06 (exit/impairment), 4.02 (non-reliance/restatement notice), 5.02 (officer departure), 7.01 (Reg FD), 8.01 (Other Events), 9.01 (Exhibits). The **DEF 14A** has standard sections including Notice/Letter, Proxy Summary, Director Election, Corporate Governance, Audit Committee, Executive Compensation (CD&A, **Pay Versus Performance since 2022 under Item 402(v)**, **CEO Pay Ratio since 2018 under Item 402(u)**), Security Ownership, Related-Party Transactions, Stockholder Proposals.

### The section-level architecture of signal

Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen's per-section decomposition assigns the dominant signal to **MD&A** (where ~75% of textual change concentrates) and the strongest *predictive* power per unit of change to **Risk Factors** (the 188 bps/month section-specific result). Item 5 (Market for Registrant's Stock) and other administrative sections contribute almost nothing. MD&A is the densest signal section because it is forward-looking, narrative, and voiced by management; Risk Factors are second because changing risk language reflects new known unknowns. The growing length problem is acute: Risk Factors sections have ballooned from 3–5 pages (2005) to 30–50+ pages (2020+); the SEC has periodically tried to require summary sections without much effect.

### How 8-K and proxies should modulate the periodic signal

This is the user's specific research question. The literature converges on four design options:

**Option (i) — single drift corpus.** Reject. 8-Ks are telegraphic event descriptions; proxies are governance/compensation narratives; 10-Ks are comprehensive operational reports. Mixing degrades both lexical and embedding similarity because cross-genre variance dominates within-firm-period drift.

**Option (ii) — flag "expected drift" periods around 8-Ks.** Lerman-Livnat (2010, *RAS* 15(4): 752–778) establish that 8-Ks and periodic filings are *complementary*, not redundant — the information content of 10-Ks did not decline post-2004 modernization. Niessner (2015) on strategic disclosure timing finds managers cluster bad-news 8-Ks on Fridays and that under-reaction persists ~3 weeks. The right design treats 8-K event flags (Items 1.01 material agreement, 2.01 acquisition, 4.02 non-reliance, 5.02 officer departure, 1.05 cyber) as predictors of *expected* subsequent 10-K drift; the 10-K drift's residual after regressing on these flags is the genuine inattention signal.

**Option (iii) — orthogonal evidence.** In addition. Compute proxy drift, 8-K density/content drift, and 10-K drift as independent series; expose all three; let downstream layers combine.

**Option (iv) — conditioning variables.** Include 8-K event types and counts since last filing as features in the *output normalization*: sector × size × regime quantile ranks conditional on 8-K-event regime.

**Recommended composite design**: compute three separate drift series — `drift_10k`, `drift_10q`, `drift_proxy` — with a side-table of 8-K events in the interval between filing pairs, broken out by item code. Residualize within size × sector × year cohorts and against 8-K event flags before computing percentile ranks. Keep `drift_score_raw` for diagnostics. This handles both the Niessner timing insight and the Lerman-Livnat complementarity finding.

For proxies specifically, Larcker-McCall-Ormazabal (2012, *JAE*) document that say-on-pay text is endogenously drifted to court ISS/Glass Lewis recommendations; Cai-Garner-Walkling (2009, *JoF* 64(5): 2389–2421) show directors not receiving an ISS positive recommendation receive ~19% fewer shareholder votes. New CEO via proxy contest reliably produces large but expected drift in the next 10-K/10-Q. Pay Versus Performance (Item 402(v), first compliance 2023 proxy season) and CEO Pay Ratio (Item 402(u), first compliance 2018) are universal regulatory step-changes. Sautner et al. (2023) and the Conference Board (2025) data on the **40% → 25% → 6% drop in S&P 100 use of "ESG" in sustainability report titles 2023→2024→2025** — driven by political backlash, not policy change — is the textbook example of vocabulary-swap failure mode that proxy-text drift must control for.

### Why drift means different things by company type

Microcap drift is high-noise — operations are smaller, shifts more material, and outside-counsel turnover is higher. Megacap drift is lower-baseline — when it occurs it is more meaningful per unit. New IPO firms have no drift baseline (need 2+ filings; Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen drop year-1 firms). Mature firms with stable boilerplate templates have artificially low drift; growth firms naturally drift more. Sector heterogeneity matters: regulated industries (banks under Reg YY/Basel III/CCAR; utilities with rate cases; biotech with pipeline updates) have more boilerplate; tech and consumer firms exhibit more managerial discretion in language. The right normalization is **size-decile × GICS-sector × year** percentile rank.

## 3. Data sources and engineering

### EDGAR canonical surface

The four endpoints that matter are the **submissions JSON API** (`https://data.sec.gov/submissions/CIK{padded}.json`), the **Archives** for direct file fetch (`https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/{cik}/{accession-no-dashes}/{primary_doc}`), the **full-index quarterly listings** (`https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/full-index/{YYYY}/QTR{N}/`), and the **EDGAR Full-Text Search EFTS** (`https://efts.sec.gov/LATEST/search-index`, indexes from 2001). The submissions JSON paginates at ~1,000 entries via the `files` array (`CIK0000320193-submissions-001.json` etc). Rate limit is **10 req/sec per IP, hard-coded since July 27, 2021**, applied across www.sec.gov, data.sec.gov, and efts.sec.gov; **User-Agent header is required** and must be a descriptive string like `"Sample Company Name AdminContact@samplecompany.com"`. 429 responses come back as 403 Forbidden with ~10-minute cooldown; recommended target is 8 req/s with exponential backoff, max 5 retries, never >1 worker per IP.

File formats by era: TXT (1993–~2001, SGML-wrapped multi-document files with ASCII tables); HTML (2002–2008, often tag-soup with inline styles, no semantic HTML); XBRL exhibits (2009+, mandatory for large filers); **iXBRL inline XBRL phased in June 2019–June 2021** (large accelerated 6/15/2019; accelerated 6/15/2020; non-accelerated 6/15/2021; FPI IFRS through 2022); **Cybersecurity CYD taxonomy** required iXBRL block-text + detail tagging for Item 1C beginning second-cycle filings. EDGAR digital coverage starts 1993Q1; mandatory electronic filing fully phased by May 1996; 8-K modernization August 23, 2004 expanded items from ~9 to 22 (current 1.01–9.01) and cut filing window to 4 business days — **a hard discontinuity for any cross-2004 8-K diffing**. Amendments (10-K/A, 10-Q/A, 8-K/A, 20-F/A, DEF 14A/A) are separate filings with separate accession numbers; there is no formal "amends accession X" field in submissions JSON, so the standard heuristic is matching `cik + periodOfReport + form_type ± /A`. Treat amendments as separate observations; never overwrite the original.

### Python libraries — concrete recommendations as of April 2026

**edgartools** (github.com/dgunning/edgartools, 5.8.x, active March 2026 commits) is the production-grade primary library: built-in rate limiting, MCP server, XBRL standardization, structured form objects (TenK, TenQ, EightK, DEF14A, 13F, Form 4) with `.income_statement`, `.risk_factors`, `.mda`, `.items` accessors, lxml + PyArrow under the hood. **Pin a recent version**; open issue #454 (Oct 2025) reports `TenK.get_item_with_part` failing on certain Part II 10-Q chunks — unit-test on a sample of 200 random accession numbers. **sec-edgar-downloader** (jadchaar, 5.1.0 Feb 2026, slow cadence but maintained) is the canonical bulk downloader, uses `pyrate-limiter` for 10 req/s. **sec-api.io** (commercial, $55–$239/month tiers) provides the **ExtractorAPI for Item-level extraction back to 1994 including legacy TXT** — the single most valuable feature for historical drift work that is hard to replicate in-house; ~98% accuracy on 19 sections per their docs. **Loughran-McDonald `Generic_Parser.py`** at sraf.nd.edu/textual-analysis/code/ is the academic reference parser; mirror its cleaning steps even if you don't run it.

For HTML parsing use **lxml.html** primary with **BeautifulSoup4 + html5lib** fallback for ~5% of pre-2010 malformed filings; **selectolax** is a faster lxml alternative worth benchmarking. For Loughran-McDonald sentiment download the **2024 Master Dictionary CSV** directly from sraf.nd.edu (`Loughran-McDonald_MasterDictionary_2024.csv`) and parse with their `MOD_Load_MasterDictionary_v2024.py` (~100 LOC, embed in your repo); **pysentiment2** lags the SRAF dictionary; **pysentiment** is abandoned. The **LM 10-X corpus** (`Loughran-McDonald_10X_DocumentDictionaries_1993-2025.txt`, 15.8 GB; `_10X_Summaries_1993-2025.csv`, 188 MB precomputed sentiment + readability per filing) is **the gold-standard baseline corpus** — use it to sanity-check your parser and provide a no-cost LM-style baseline.

For embeddings the top recommendations on a 4090 with 24GB are: **`yiyanghkust/finbert-tone`** (768-dim, sentiment-fine-tuned, the most-validated finance encoder); **`yiyanghkust/finbert-pretrain`** (un-fine-tuned base for representation work); **`sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2`** (768-dim, 87–88 STS-B, the workhorse general-purpose baseline at ~800–1500 sentences/sec FP16 batch 64–128); **`BAAI/bge-m3`** (1024-dim, 8192 ctx, dense+sparse+ColBERT-style multivector); **`jinaai/jina-embeddings-v3`** (1024-dim Matryoshka 32–1024, 8192 ctx, task-specific LoRA adapters including `text-matching` ideal for drift cosine, ~150 long-passages/sec FP16). **Fin-E5** (Tang et al. 2025) is current SOTA on FinMTEB but requires e5-mistral-7b-instruct base and is more demanding. **BloombergGPT** is closed-source and frozen at v3 December 2023; mention for completeness, not deployable. For chunking use `langchain.text_splitter.RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter` as fallback and `pysbd` for sentence segmentation (better than NLTK for finance prose with $, %, abbreviations). For diff/similarity use `sklearn.feature_extraction.text.TfidfVectorizer` + `cosine_similarity`, `rapidfuzz` for Levenshtein/token-set, `datasketch.MinHash + MinHashLSH` for scalable Jaccard, and `faiss-gpu` for ANN over embedding vectors (`IndexFlatIP` for ≤1M, `IndexIVFPQ` for full corpus).

### Commercial data providers

**WRDS SEC Analytics Suite** (Wharton) is the academic gold standard: 12M+ filing index, 3.5M+ parsed text/HTML, **WRDS SEC Readability and Sentiment** dataset with 11 filing characteristics, 8 readability measures, 16 sentiment measures including LM/Harvard-GI/Henry/Diction, 2 TB cleaned filings updated daily, with survivor-bias-free CIK↔GVKEY↔CUSIP linking. Direct individual subscription has a **15 GB/month data cap that makes bulk download impractical**; only academic site licenses provide unrestricted access. **AlphaSense** (which acquired Tegus for $930M July 2024 and Sentieo earlier; raised $650M Series F at $4B valuation) provides a "Document Comparison" tool that is the closest commercial product to what we are building — useful for benchmarking but $12k–$30k/seat/year. **Calcbench** (~$6k–$15k/year) has a Disclosures product with section-level diffs; cheaper alternative. **Bloomberg, Refinitiv, S&P Capital IQ, FactSet** all carry filings but at $13k–$30k/user/year and primarily aimed at human users, not bulk research. For an academic project use **WRDS + LM corpus + free EDGAR**; for production quant use **raw EDGAR + sec-api.io ExtractorAPI** as the cheapest viable stack.

### Parsing challenges specific to filings

**Pre-2002 TXT-only filings** (~6 years × ~30k 10-Ks = ~180k filings) are SGML-wrapped with ASCII tables; Item headers vary as `ITEM 1.`, `Item 1 -`, `ITEM I — BUSINESS`, even `I T E M  1` — expect ~5% parser failure even with LM's `Generic_Parser.py`. **Section detection** via regex on `Item N` headers fails on (a) pre-2002 spacing, (b) "Item 7. and 7A. — Combined", (c) Item 5 incorporating Item 6, (d) TOC duplicating headers, (e) tables of contents listing different page anchors than the actual section. ML-based section classification is recommended where Loughran-McDonald's 10-X parsed corpus or **EDGAR-CRAWLER** (Loukas et al., WWW 2025) can provide labels.

**Boilerplate that dominates token counts**: PSLRA Section 27A forward-looking-statement safe harbor (200–500 words, near-verbatim YoY, ~3–5% of token mass with ~0% drift signal); Risk Factor introductory disclaimers; "About Us" / "Our Company" in Item 1; **Item 1C Cybersecurity in early years is heavily template-driven** (Sidley, Gibson Dunn, MoFo memos circulated before the 2024 deadline — Year 1→Year 2 cyber drift will be dominated by template substitution, not real change); Mine Safety (Item 4) is "Not applicable" for ~95% of filers — drop or mask. **Exhibits to filter** for drift: Exhibit 21 subsidiaries list, Exhibit 23 auditor consents, Exhibits 31/32 SOX certifications, Exhibit 99 press releases, Exhibit 97 clawback (new 2023), Exhibit 19 insider trading policy (new 2023). For drift restrict to **Items 1, 1A, 1C, 7, 7A** in the 10-K and **Part I + Part II Item 1A** in the 10-Q.

**Cross-references / "incorporated by reference":** 10-K Items 10–14 are almost always incorporated from the DEF 14A. The 10-K text for these items is essentially zero prose; the real content lives in the proxy. Excluding Items 10–14 from 10-K drift comparisons is essential; alternatively, pull the corresponding DEF 14A sections and concatenate. A firm switching from incorporation-by-reference to direct disclosure (or vice versa) shows up as massive drift even with no information change. **Foreign filers** use Form 20-F not 10-K (different items entirely), often in IFRS, with 200–500 page typical length — cannot mix 10-K and 20-F drift baselines. **Smaller Reporting Company** scaled disclosure means Item 7A is not required; do not penalize as missing section. **Transition reports** (10-KT, 10-QT) cover non-standard periods — detect via form type and either align period or exclude.

### Corpus economics

Annual filing volumes (recent years): **10-K ~5,500–7,000**; 10-K/A ~300–500; **10-Q ~17,000–20,000**; 10-Q/A ~500–800; **8-K ~110,000–135,000**; 8-K/A ~3,000; **DEF 14A ~4,500–5,500**; **20-F ~700–900**; 6-K ~25,000+. Cumulative since 1993: ~21M filings of all types, of which the narrative-rich set (10-K + 10-Q + 8-K + 20-F + DEF 14A + S-1 + S-4) is ~3–4M filings. Raw narrative-only corpus 1993–present is ~800 GB–1.5 TB raw HTML/TXT, **~150–300 GB after stripping HTML tags / exhibits / images to clean text**. Allocate ~500 GB for full historical narrative; ~2 TB for comfortable working set.

Embedding storage budget at 768-dim float32 (3 KB/vector) for 32 years of narrative corpus: section-level ~24M vectors ≈ **74 GB FP32 / 37 GB FP16 / 575 MB PQ16-compressed**; paragraph-level ~144M ≈ **440 GB / 220 GB / 3.4 GB**; sentence-level ~1.05B ≈ **3.2 TB / 1.6 TB / 25 GB**. Compute on RTX 4090 FP16: full backfill of section-level embeddings ~44 GPU-hours; paragraph-level ~13 hours; sentence-level ~55 hours but should be scope-restricted. **The practical compromise is section-level mean-pooled embeddings with paragraph-level only for flagged candidates.** Storage backend: parquet (one row per chunk: cik, accession, item, chunk_id, text, embedding) on local SSD, with **DuckDB** as the query engine (handles billions of rows on a 4090 box) and faiss IVF-PQ for vectors.

## 4. Three drift methodologies, equal depth, evidence-based recommendation

### Lexical similarity

**Jaccard** is set overlap J(A,B) = |A∩B|/|A∪B| over token (or n-gram) sets, with weighted variant J_w = Σmin(x_i,y_i)/Σmax(x_i,y_i) for term-frequency vectors. Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen actually compute four measures: cosine on raw term-frequency vectors of the union vocabulary, Jaccard, normalized minimum-edit distance, and a "simple" word-track-changes ratio Sim_Simple = 1 − (additions+deletions+changes)/(|D1|+|D2|). All four generate similar alphas in their main tables; Jaccard is marginally stronger in High-Litigiousness and High-Uncertainty subsamples. The point is not that Jaccard wins decisively — it is that *simple bag-of-words measures are sufficient* to extract the signal. For implementation, **MinHash with 128 permutations** (datasketch) gives O(N) Jaccard at corpus scale; pure set Jaccard is more length-sensitive than cosine because |A∪B| grows faster than |A∩B| as docs lengthen, so raw Jaccard scores are not comparable across years without yearly normalization.

**Cosine similarity over TF-IDF**: TF-IDF vector v_d[t] = tf(t,d)·idf(t), L2-normalized, with sklearn smoothed default idf(t) = log((1+N)/(1+df(t))) + 1; cosine on L2-normalized vectors is mathematically invariant to multiplicative scaling. The single most-asked interview decision: **where do you fit IDF?** Three options: (1) fit on full corpus, IDFs stable, comparable across firms — **the right default**, what Hoberg-Phillips and most academic work do; (2) fit on a single firm's filings, captures firm-specific term importance but unstable; (3) fit per pair, which is degenerate (effectively plain TF cosine, which is what Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen actually do — do not confuse this with true TF-IDF). Use `TfidfVectorizer(sublinear_tf=True, lowercase=True, ngram_range=(1,1), min_df=5, max_df=0.95, stop_words="english", norm="l2", dtype=np.float32)`, **fit once on the full historical corpus and freeze**, transform each new filing. N-gram choice: unigrams primary, (1,2) variant in parallel for ablation; (1,3) has diminishing returns and explodes vocab.

**Section-level vs whole-document vs sliding-window**: whole-document is what Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen do but dilutes section-specific signal; section-level (compute per-Item cosine, then weight) is recommended because their own decomposition shows MD&A and Risk Factors drive nearly all the signal; sliding-window is rarely needed if section extraction works.

**Strengths**: interpretable, deterministic, reproducible across software versions (TF-IDF is a math identity), fast (full corpus run on a 64GB workstation in 1–2 hours), no GPU, robust to OCR noise, audit-friendly for compliance. **Weaknesses**: synonym substitution invisible (`risk → peril` reads as 100% changed); semantically blind; boilerplate dominates similarity unless `sublinear_tf=True` and high `max_df` pruning are applied; word order ignored.

### Embedding-based similarity

**Sentence-BERT family**: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (384-dim, ~14k sentences/sec on a 4090, STS-B ~84–85), all-mpnet-base-v2 (768-dim, ~800–1500 sentences/sec FP16, STS-B ~87–88, the SBERT docs explicitly call it the best-quality bi-encoder). Cross-encoders give higher accuracy but O(N²) cost — useful only for re-ranking pre-filtered pairs.

**The critical FinBERT distinction** that most teams misuse in interviews: **ProsusAI/finbert** (Araci 2019) is a 3-class softmax sentiment classifier, not an embedding model; pulling `[CLS]` from `BertModel.from_pretrained('ProsusAI/finbert')` works as an encoder but the representation has been shaped by sentiment fine-tuning. **yiyanghkust/finbert-tone** is the same caveat at a different fine-tuning step. **yiyanghkust/finbert-pretrain** is the un-fine-tuned base on a 4.9B-token finance corpus (10-K/10-Q 2.5B + earnings calls 1.3B + analyst reports 1.1B) — **this is the right choice for a finance-domain encoder rather than a classifier**. **FinLang/finance-embeddings-investopedia** is fine-tuned on top of BAAI/bge-base-en-v1.5 with LLM-distilled Investopedia Q&A pairs, but is CC-BY-NC license — commercial use restricted. **FinBERT2** (arXiv 2506.06335, 2025) shows +6.8% over BGE-base-zh and +4.2% over OpenAI text-embedding-3-large on financial retrieval but is mostly Chinese-focused.

**Doc2Vec PV-DBOW and PV-DM** trainable on the firm's 10-K archive captures domain-specific co-occurrence; deterministic given hyperparameters, no licensing issues. Empirically inferior to SBERT on STS but defensible because trained on your data — the same path Hoberg-Phillips took for their 2025 *JoF* update.

**Long-context models** (Longformer 4096, BigBird 4096, Mamba-based, Llama-3-Long): cost trade-offs are sharp. A 4090 with 24GB runs Longformer-base at batch 4 on 4096 tokens at maybe 10–20 docs/sec; modern Apple 10-K is ~80k tokens. **The deployed solution is chunking** to 256–384 tokens at sentence/paragraph level with all-mpnet-base-v2, then aggregating; long-context models are a research deflection.

**Modern general embeddings** competitive with finance-domain include BGE-M3, Nomic Embed v2, Voyage-3, OpenAI text-embedding-3-large, Jina-embeddings-v3. The decisive evidence is FinMTEB (Tang et al. 2025): **performance on general-purpose benchmarks shows limited correlation with financial domain tasks; domain-adapted models consistently outperform; and surprisingly, a simple bag-of-words approach outperforms sophisticated dense embeddings in financial STS tasks**. The companion arXiv 2409.18511 ("Do We Need Domain-Specific Embedding Models?") shows general-purpose embeddings degrade significantly on FinMTEB vs MTEB, attributable to benchmark differences not architecture.

**Aggregation strategies**: mean-pool of sentence embeddings is the cheap baseline; max-pool emphasizes salient sentences but is noisier; attention-weighted-pool needs labels; **section-importance-weighted pool** (analyst prior: MD&A 1.0, Risk Factors 0.8, Item 1 0.4, exhibits 0.0) is the recommended default; hierarchical (sentence → paragraph → section → doc) is the standard for 10-K. **Comparison metrics**: direct cosine on doc-level embedding (lossy because averaging hides local changes); section-level cosine then weighted mean (recommended primary); Earth Mover's Distance / Optimal Transport between sentence-embedding distributions captures rewording structure but is O(n³) per pair, tractable only on critical sections; BERTScore-style alignment is a cheaper greedy alternative for paragraph-level alignment.

**Strengths**: paraphrase-robust, semantic, captures domain-specific shifts when domain encoder is used. **Weaknesses**: opaque (drift score is magnitude-only; no human-readable explanation of *what* changed); slow; GPU-required; encoder versions drift over time (model card updates → reproducibility problem unless model SHA is pinned); OOV/jargon issues with general models.

### Specific-language-feature drift

The signal here is the **change in feature value between filing_t and filing_{t-1}**, not the level. Layer 4 already handles level; Layer 1 reads as Δfeature.

**Loughran-McDonald sentiment shift** uses the 2024 Master Dictionary's seven categories (Negative ~2,355, Positive ~354, Uncertainty ~297, Litigious ~903, Strong-Modal, Weak-Modal, Constraining ~184) with the new Complexity column. Weighting choices: raw frequency (length-sensitive), proportion (preferred for cross-sectional comparability), or LM 2011's TF-IDF-with-doc-length normalization. Drift formulation: for each category c, Δ_c = prop_c(t) − prop_c(t−1); stack as a vector and use ||Δ||_2 as one summary, but **keep all 7 deltas as separate features** for downstream ML. **Pin to `LoughranMcDonald_MasterDictionary_2024.csv` and document the SHA**; pysentiment2 ships an older version.

**Modal-word analysis**: drift toward weak-modal language (may, might, could) relative to strong-modal (will, shall, must) is the canonical managerial-uncertainty tell. Track [Δ(strong_prop), Δ(weak_prop), Δ(weak_prop − strong_prop)]. **Hedging language** (Hyland 2005 list of ~100 hedges; Bochkay et al. 2020 finance-adapted) overlaps substantially with LM Uncertainty + Weak-Modal — treat as supplementary.

**Readability metrics**: Fog Index (Li 2008), Flesch-Kincaid, SMOG, ARI, Coleman-Liau via `textstat`. **Loughran-McDonald 2014 demolish Fog for finance — log(file size) is the recommended primary readability proxy**. Year-over-year change in file size or Fog is a defensible single-feature drift signal; Bog Index (Bonsall et al. 2017) is better still but proprietary. **Litigation-language drift** (LM Litigious): Rogers et al. (*AR* 2011) and Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen find growth in litigation language predicts subsequent legal events. **Forward-looking-statement frequency drift**: detect via regex over sentences with lookahead words (`expect|anticipate|believe|intend|plan|forecast|project|outlook|will|may|should`) or use yiyanghkust/finbert-fls classifier (more accurate but slower). **Bodnaruk-Loughran-McDonald 2015 constraining-words list** is a direct LM extension. **Strengths**: highly interpretable (you can hand a regulator the word list); mechanistically grounded in 15+ years of accounting literature; trivial compute (<1 sec/filing); each feature independently validated against returns and earnings outcomes; **lets you explain *what* changed**, not just that something changed — the killer feature embeddings lack. **Weaknesses**: dictionary methods miss context (negation: "no material weakness" still scores as litigious); easily gamed once IR teams learn the formula (Cao-Jiang-Yang-Zhang 2023 RFS endogeneity finding); dictionaries lag terminology.

### Hybrid approaches

Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen's two-method (cosine + Jaccard) is itself a partial hybrid; redundancy buys robustness, not new signal. The **Hoberg-Lewis 2017 abnormal-disclosure residual** is the right architectural pattern: regress similarity_t on log(length_t), industry FE, year FE, and use the residual as `abnormal_drift`. This handles the secular length growth directly. **Section weighting** calibrated empirically with reasonable priors of MD&A 1.0, Risk Factors 0.8, Legal 0.6, Item 1 Business 0.4, exhibits/safe-harbor 0.0. **Ensemble strategy**: start with z-score-and-average across `lex_cosine`, `lex_jaccard`, `emb_cosine`, `lm_l2norm`, `log_filesize_delta` (no labels needed); upgrade to learned linear combination via logistic regression on a labeled subsample (AAERs, restatements, future returns binarized) once you have enough labels; **gradient-boosted ensembles risk overfitting** to whatever label was chosen — use as stretch goal only with cross-validated holdouts.

### Operational details

**Section extraction** is the single biggest engineering risk in Layer 1. Regex on `Item N` headers fails on the cases listed in Section 3. ML-based section classification trained on Loughran-McDonald 10-X parsed corpus or EDGAR-CRAWLER labels reaches ~90–95% on 10-K, lower on 10-Q. sec-api.io ExtractorAPI reaches ~98% on 19 sections — operational standard if budget allows. **Filter to clean U.S. domestic 10-K and 10-Q for Layer 1 v1**; expand to 20-F, DEF 14A, and 8-K in v2.

**The 10-K-to-10-K vs 10-K-to-10-Q comparison problem**: 10-K vs prior fiscal year is the cleanest comparison; 10-Q vs same-fiscal-quarter prior year handles seasonality (Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen approach); cross-form-type comparisons (10-K vs 10-Q) introduce structural noise — avoid unless adjusting by section overlap. For amendments, replace the original with the most-recent amended version for comparison purposes (last-filed wins), but flag `is_amendment` and `amends_accession`.

**Compute targets**: lexical methods on 200K-filing corpus are CPU-feasible in 1–2 hours; LM features in <30 min; readability in <30 min; MiniLM-L6 sentence embedding (~3·10⁸ sentences) on RTX 4090 FP16 is ~14 hours; mpnet-base sentence embedding ~3 days; section-level mean-pooled embeddings on 200K × 20 sections is <1 day with ~6 GB storage. **Lexical + LM features run end-to-end on a laptop in a long evening; sentence-level embeddings on the full corpus are 4090-feasible but take days.**

**Score normalization** pipeline order: raw → residualize on (length, industry, year) → yearly z-score → sector × size percentile rank → final score. Always export all three (raw, yearly z, sector-size percentile) so downstream consumers can choose.

### Recommendation

**Despite the layer's name, the evidence supports lexical-first, LM-feature-augmented, embedding-secondary architecture.** The reasoning chain: (1) Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen extract the entire Lazy Prices alpha using bag-of-words; (2) FinMTEB explicitly finds bag-of-words beats dense embeddings on financial STS; (3) Liu-Yang-Tam shows off-the-shelf embeddings fail on subtle drift detection; (4) lexical methods are deterministic, version-stable, runnable on a laptop, and explainable to regulators while embeddings introduce model-version risk and opacity; (5) embeddings still earn their place for paraphrase-robustness — exactly the case where lexical fails; (6) LM features earn their place because they let you explain *what* changed.

**Tier-1 (build first)**: `LexCosineScorer` (TF-IDF cosine on MD&A + Risk Factors, full-corpus IDF, sublinear_tf, unigrams, output 1−cos); `LexJaccardScorer` (MinHash-based Jaccard); `LMFeatureDriftScorer` (vector of Δ across 7 LM categories, primary aggregate = L2 norm); `ReadabilityDriftScorer` (Δ log file size primary, Fog secondary diagnostic only). All four residualized against length/industry/year, then yearly z-scored. **Tier-2 (add second, complementary)**: `EmbDriftScorer` using all-mpnet-base-v2 at section-level mean-pool, MD&A + Risk Factors weighted aggregate; `FinBERT-tone-CLS drift` for sentiment-axis drift specifically. **Tier-3 (research, optional)**: Optimal Transport over sentence embeddings on flagged MD&A only; Doc2Vec trained on the firm corpus; FinBERT2 or BGE-M3 once pinned versions stabilize; Liu-Yang-Tam-style LLM-augmented contrastive pipeline if labels become available.

## 5. Validation strategy

### Replication targets and expected magnitudes

The Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen replication should sort the universe (Russell 3000 or CRSP common-stock NYSE/NASDAQ/AMEX, excluding price <$5 and ADR-only) monthly by drift quintile, hold 3 months, rebalance monthly with one-month lag, weight value-weight as primary and equal-weight as secondary. Expected post-shrinkage realistic target after Bayesian adjustment for ~50–70% post-publication decay (McLean-Pontiff 2016 framework, supported by Offutt-Xie 2025 replication finding ~7%/year value-weighted abnormal returns vs 22% headline): **3–8% annualized FF5 alpha gross of transaction costs**, ~100 bps after frictions at $1B notional. Below 3% net of costs, the signal is a wash; above 5% net is acceptance.

### Known-pattern backtests

**Negative cases (high drift expected ahead of failure):** **Enron 1999 → 2000 → 2001** 10-Ks should show progressive drift in Related Party Transactions, off-balance-sheet partnerships (LJM, Raptor SPEs), and MD&A; the 2000 10-K is the classic test case with heavy variable-interest-entity language. **WorldCom 2001–2002**: capex-vs-opex language and goodwill discussion; Q1 2002 10-Q drift before the June 2002 restatement. **Lehman Brothers 2007–2008** Risk Factors and MD&A — the 2007 10-K filed Feb 2008 vs 2006 is the key pair, with documented Repo 105 disclosure absence and progressive opacity in liquidity narrative (Examiner's Report 2010). **SVB Financial 2022 10-K vs 2021 10-K** filed Feb 24, 2023 (12 days before March 8 collapse) is the strongest recent test — increased Risk Factor word count paired with decreased sentiment per Context Analytics; AAA's *Current Issues in Auditing* 18(2): C11 confirms risk-factor disclosures were "rather generic" relative to similarly positioned First Republic. **Hertz** April 2020 going-concern language; **J&J/talc** progressive Risk Factor and Legal Proceedings expansion 2017+ with LTL Management bankruptcy strategy 2021. Wirecard is FPI-only (Bundesanzeiger, not SEC) so out of scope unless corpus extends. **Positive controls**: Apple, Microsoft, Berkshire — all expected near-zero drift z-score; an instructive exception is Berkshire 2023 10-K with Munger memorial and succession discussion as a real-but-benign drift example.

### Out-of-sample portfolio backtests

Risk-adjustment regressions: CAPM, Fama-French 3, Carhart 4, Fama-French 5, FF5 + MOM, Hou-Xue-Zhang Q-factor. **Section-weighted variants**: MD&A-only (CMN's strongest section), Risk Factors-only (Filzen 2015 and Florackis et al. 2023 supportive), CEO/CFO-language drift, litigation-language drift, composite. **Crisis robustness**: 2008 GFC ensure Q1 changers don't capture failed banks (selection on outcome — exclude GFC and signal should attenuate but persist); 2020 COVID is a near-universal common-drift event — exclude Mar–Jun 2020 and residualize on a pandemic-language topic vector; 2022 simultaneous inflation/rate/Russia-Ukraine/SVB shocks require within-sector and within-size cohort tests. **Transaction cost reasonableness** per Frazzini-Israel-Moskowitz (2018): Russell 3000 monthly rebalance with 200% turnover/year incurs roughly 30–80 bps/year spread+impact at $1B notional.

### Null hypothesis tests

**Random-time-shuffle**: permute filing order within firm, recompute drift, run identical sort. Alpha must collapse to near zero; if it does not, drift is capturing cross-sectional features (size, sector, length) rather than temporal signal. **Random-section-pair controls**: pair Item 7 year-t with Item 1A year-(t-1); drift should be uncorrelated with future returns — if not, signal is contaminated by section-length confound. **Length-controlled comparisons within length-decile bins**: signal should persist within bin; if it concentrates in extreme length-change bins, length is the signal not drift. **Placebo on filing date**: assign returns from a randomly chosen 1-year-displaced date; alpha should vanish. **Boilerplate placebo**: restrict to firms with >90% MD&A unchanged (exact-string Jaccard ≥ 0.9); drift score within this universe should produce no signal.

### Embedding-specific validation

**GPT-4 paraphrase synthetic pairs**: paraphrase each section preserving meaning, calibrate the cosine distance threshold so paraphrase-pair distances form the lower tail of the null distribution; drift below that threshold is indistinguishable from same-meaning rewrite. **Liu-Yang-Tam Financial-STS benchmark** (NAACL Findings 2024, FinSTS dataset) and **FinMTEB** (Tang et al. 2025 EMNLP, including FinSTS and FINAL) as held-out validation; the FinMTEB BoW-beats-dense finding is the strong null hypothesis our embedding choice must beat. Yang-Yang-Mai (2024 IEEE CIFEr) FinSTS dataset of 4,000 GPT-4-active-learning-annotated sentence pairs is a separate, complementary benchmark.

## 6. Failure modes

The **fourteen named failure modes** the layer must defend against, each with specific mechanism and proposed defense:

**Length bias** as 10-Ks grew from ~30K (1995) to 80K+ (2024) words: cosine on TF-IDF approximately length-invariant in the limit but raw token counts and Jaccard are not, and Brown-Tucker (2011) prove cosine converges to 1 as shared common-word vocabulary grows. Defense: residualize on log(length_t) and log(length_t/length_{t-1}) per Brown-Tucker; report length_change_normalized as a separate column.

**Boilerplate dominance** with PSLRA Section 27A safe-harbor language ~3–5% of token mass and ~0% drift signal; same for Reg G non-GAAP reconciliations, ICFR statements, auditor signature blocks. Defense: maintain a curated boilerplate stop-list at the n-gram level, excised before similarity computation, re-curated annually.

**Auditor change** triggering automatic Item 9A and audit-report block changes from a Big-4 → Big-4 switch produces ~5% Jaccard hit without business change. Defense: flag `has_auditor_change` from 8-K Item 4.01 history; mask Item 9A from drift when the flag is on.

**Regulatory step-changes** are the most-important failure-mode class because each creates *common* drift across thousands of firms in a single year, dominating per-firm signal. The list to track: **Cybersecurity Item 1C and Item 1.05** (final July 2023, FY2023+ for 10-K and Dec 18 2023 for 8-K); **Climate disclosure rule** (March 6, 2024 final, voluntarily stayed April 4, 2024, SEC voted to stop defending April 2025 — bidirectional drift as some firms added climate language in 2023–2024 and are now removing it); **Pay Versus Performance** (Item 402(v), 2023 proxy season for calendar-year filers); **CEO Pay Ratio** (Item 402(u), 2018 first compliance); **Item 6 reclassification** (Feb 10, 2021 — Selected Financial Data eliminated, ~40% of firms eliminated outright, creating phantom −100% drift in Item 6 if not handled); **HFCAA Item 9C** (Dec 2021 final, all 10-Ks gained Item 9C heading typically reading "None" except for ~174 China-affiliated CIIs); SEC Director Gerding's May 2024 clarification that voluntary cyber disclosures should use Item 8.01 not 1.05 caused Item 1.05 filings to drop from 17 to 9 while Item 8.01 cyber filings rose from 6 to 28 — a regulatory-language step-change in mid-2024. Defense: maintain a regulatory-regime calendar indexed by effective date and applicable filer cohort; populate boolean `regime_flags` per filing; residualize within-year drift signals against active flags or compute drift only on the intersection of sections present in both periods.

**Acquired-language drift** when M&A targets' business descriptions, risk factors, segments, accounting policies appear in subsequent 10-Ks (2018 BB&T → 2020 Truist post-merger 10-K is mostly new material). Defense: flag `had_material_acquisition_in_period` from 8-K 2.01 events with deal size >5–10% of acquirer market cap; treat as conditioning variable.

**Restatement and amendment**: 10-K/A or 10-Q/A amendments differ from originals by definition. Defense: always compare original-to-original, with amendments stored separately; flag `is_amendment` and `amends_accession`.

**Section reorganization** when companies move climate discussion from MD&A "Trends" to a new "Sustainability" subsection in Item 1. Defense: rely on semantic section identification (topic-model or LLM-classified) rather than literal SEC item numbering whenever possible; or compute whole-document drift in addition to section-restricted drift and require both to fire.

**Filing-vendor / law-firm template changes** when outside counsel switch (Wachtell → S&C) or filing-services switch (Donnelley → Toppan Merrill → Workiva) brings different style templates. Defense: flag `vendor_template_change_suspected` if drift score is high but litigation/MD&A subsection drift is low; no clean residualization possible.

**Coverage gap / parser failure** on small-cap and microcap filings using scanned-PDF formats, non-standard exhibits, or breaking BeautifulSoup HTML extraction. Defense: parser confidence score; drop filings with parse-quality flag below threshold; report `parse_quality_score` and `parse_method` provenance.

**Embedding drift over time** when hosted embedding APIs (OpenAI, Voyage, Cohere) update mid-period, comparing pre-update vs post-update embeddings produces fake drift on unchanged text (text-embedding-ada-002 → text-embedding-3-large transition). Defense: freeze model and version; record `embedding_model_id` and `embedding_model_version_sha` per row; re-embed entire historical corpus on any model change; never mix versions.

**Cross-language 20-Fs** from non-English-native firms exhibit translation-artifact patterns. Defense: treat 20-F separately from 10-K corpus; train/evaluate FPI drift in its own pool with FPI-specific normalization.

**Algorithmic-listening endogeneity** per Cao-Jiang-Yang-Zhang (2023, *RFS* 36(9): 3603–3642): firms have been gaming text signals since at least 2011, increasingly so post-2018, explicitly more so post-2022. Defenses: prefer structural drift signals (length, section reorganization, modal-verb patterns) harder to cosmetically game than dictionary-sentiment; include `algorithmic_audience_score` (machine-download fraction, AI-investor ownership share) as a residualization variable; **expect signal decay post-2018 in any backtest**.

**ESG terminology swap** with S&P 100 use of "ESG" in sustainability report titles dropping 40% (2023) → 25% (2024) → 6% (2025) per Conference Board data — substantive disclosures continue under euphemisms ("sustainability," "responsible business," "long-term value"). Defense: maintain a synonym/relabeling dictionary updated annually; apply lemmatization or terminology normalization before similarity computation; flag `political_relabeling_year`.

**Item 6 phantom drift** is the specific case of regulatory step-change deserving explicit treatment because nearly every 10-K is affected. Defense: explicit `is_item6_reclass` regime flag for FY2021 filings; mask Item 6 section comparison in FY2020↔FY2021 transition.

## 7. Open research questions

Classified as field-level open (F), build-to-learn (B), or configurable parameter (C):

**Optimal section weighting (B/C)**: MD&A 1.0, Risk Factors 0.8 are reasonable priors but should be calibrated empirically on backtest. **Optimal embedding model (B)**: Fin-E5, FinTextSim, FinBERT-pretrain, or hybrid? The FinMTEB BoW-beats-dense finding is the strong null; embeddings must demonstrably beat lexical on internal validation before earning Tier-1 status. **8-K weighting (B)**: additive to periodic drift, conditioning gate, or "expected drift" flag — the recommended composite design treats 8-K event flags as residualization regressors and the 8-K language drift as orthogonal signal, but the empirical tradeoff is unsettled. **Proxy weighting (B)** similarly. **Drift directionality (F/B)**: most methods produce magnitude only; extracting direction (toward more uncertain, more positive, more litigious) requires the LM-feature track and is one reason it cannot be replaced by embedding-only. **Cross-firm drift correlations (F)**: industry-level drift, peer-group drift, cluster drift — Hoberg-Phillips TNIC provides the framework but no published work measures *correlated drift* explicitly. **Optimal lookback window (C)**: how many prior filings to compare against; CMN use prior 1; rolling 3-filing baselines may smooth noise. **Whether drift signal works in international markets (F)**: EU MAR-style disclosures, UK Companies House, ESMA filings have different mandate structures. **Whether the post-2014 Lazy Prices alpha persists, decays, or reverses (F)** is the single most-important field-level open question and the most-defensible internal experiment for this layer. **Endogeneity / algorithmic-listening adaptation rate (F)**: Cao-Jiang-Yang-Zhang document the phenomenon but the rate of decay post-2022 LLM era is unmeasured.

## 8. Cross-layer integration

### Layer 1 score schema

The `BaseScorer` DataFrame must include the following columns on each (cik, period_pair) row:

**Identity / join keys**: `cik` (int64, zero-padded), `ticker` (string, primary as of filing_date_b), `filing_date_a` and `filing_date_b` (dates), `period_start` and `period_end` (dates), `form_type` (enum: '10-K' / '10-Q' / 'DEF 14A' / '20-F'), `section_scope` (enum: 'whole_doc' / 'mdna' / 'risk_factors' / 'biz_descr' / 'controls' / 'compensation_cda' / others). **Composite scores**: `drift_score` (composite, sector/size-residualized, z-score-like), `drift_label` (enum: low/medium/high or quintile 1–5), `primary_drift_section` (string indicating which section drove most of the score). **Per-method scores (raw)**: `lex_cosine_score`, `lex_jaccard_score`, `emb_cosine_score`, `lm_neg_delta`, `lm_pos_delta`, `lm_unc_delta`, `lm_lit_delta`, `lm_strong_modal_delta`, `lm_weak_modal_delta`, `lm_constraining_delta`, `modal_shift`, `log_filesize_delta`, `length_change_normalized`. **Uncertainty**: `drift_uncertainty_delta` (LM uncertainty word delta specifically; note this is distinct from the bootstrap-stderr meaning). **Regime / event flags (booleans)**: `is_cyber_rule_year`, `is_climate_rule_year`, `is_pvp_year`, `is_pay_ratio_first_year`, `is_item6_reclass`, `is_hfcaa_year`, `is_amendment`, `amends_accession` (string nullable), `has_auditor_change`, `has_material_acquisition`, `is_after_8k_5_02`, `is_after_8k_4_02`, `is_after_8k_1_05`, `count_8k_2_02_in_period` (int), `count_8k_other_in_period` (int), `is_ipo_year_1`. **Provenance**: `source_filing_accession_a`, `source_filing_accession_b`, `parse_method`, `parse_quality_score`, `embedding_model_id`, `embedding_model_version_sha`, `methodology_version` (e.g., `drift_v1.4.2`), `methodology_version_sha` (git commit hash), `residualization_method` (enum: 'none' / 'sector_size_year_zscore' / 'sector_size_year_regime_resid' / 'cohort_rank'), `date_computed` (UTC timestamp). **Three normalization views, all kept**: `drift_score_raw` (unnormalized composite), `drift_score_yearly_z` (within-year z-score), `drift_score_sector_size_pctile` (percentile rank within sector × size × year cohort, bounded [0,1]).

### Layer 1 ↔ Layer 2 (Insider Divergence) join

Theoretical motivation is **Cohen, Malloy & Pomorski (2012), *Journal of Finance* 67(3): 1009–1043, "Decoding Inside Information,"** distinguishing routine (predictable, calendar-clustered) from opportunistic insider trades; the opportunistic-only portfolio earns 82 bps/month value-weighted abnormal returns. The empirical hypothesis for the cross-layer join: **concurrent high opportunistic insider sells + high filing drift = strongest "say-do gap" signal** in the framework — managers say something different in their filings while opportunistically selling. High insider buys + low/no drift = managers sticking to their story while putting personal capital in (positive). Low insider activity + high drift = signal originates from disclosure realignment, not personal-position-based conviction. Join keys: `cik`, `ticker`, `insider_trade_date BETWEEN filing_date_a AND filing_date_b`, optionally restricted to `section_scope IN ('mdna', 'risk_factors')`. Layer 2 should expose `opportunistic_buys_during_period`, `opportunistic_sells_during_period`, `routine_buys/sells_during_period`, `cluster_indicator` (Cohen-Li-Malloy classification).

### Layer 1 ↔ Layer 4 (Public Discourse) join

Theoretical motivation is **Tetlock (2007), *JoF* 62(3): 1139–1168** ("Giving Content to Investor Sentiment" — daily WSJ pessimism predicts downward price pressure followed by reversion) plus Engelberg's (2008) costly-information-processing framework and Engelberg-Reed-Ringgenberg (2012) on short-seller news skill. The empirical hypothesis: **filing drift Granger-causes news sentiment**, with predictability strongest at t+1 to t+2 weeks (immediate analyst-summary coverage and slower diffusion through trade press) and fading by t+4 weeks. Join keys: `cik`, `ticker`, `news_date BETWEEN filing_date_b AND filing_date_b + interval '8 weeks'`, grouped by week_offset. Layer 4 exposes weekly aggregated `news_sentiment`, `news_volume`, `analyst_revisions`, `social_media_sentiment` per (cik, week). Validity test: lagged regression `news_sentiment_{t+k} ~ drift_score_t + controls` for k ∈ {1,2,4,8} weeks, expecting positive significant coefficients at k=1 and k=2 for high-drift filings.

### Modulating variables — side tables, no premature aggregation

Maintained as separate side tables keyed on (cik, as_of_date) and exposed for downstream layers but **not folded into drift_score**: `dim_company_size` (market_cap, revenue_ttm, total_assets, employee_count, NYSE_size_decile); `dim_sector` (gics_sector, gics_industry_group, gics_industry, gics_subindustry, sic); `dim_governance` (e_index Bebchuk-Cohen-Ferrell, g_index Gompers-Ishii-Metrick, iss_governance_qualityscore, iss_compensation_score, iss_shareholder_rights_score, classified_board_flag); `dim_executive_turnover` (from 8-K Item 5.02 history: ceo_turnover_in_period, cfo_turnover_in_period, chair_turnover_in_period, days_since_last_turnover); `dim_insider_trading` from Layer 2; `dim_analyst_coverage` (n_analysts, dispersion, recent revisions). Each downstream user has different priors about which variables are exogenous controls vs signal modifiers; folding all into a single composite hides heterogeneity.

### Provenance and reproducibility

Every drift score must be reproducible from source filings by anyone with EDGAR archives, the methodology repo at the named SHA, and the embedding model snapshot. Mandatory per-row: `source_filing_accession_a` and `_b`, `section_scope` and `section_extraction_rule_sha`, `methodology_version` and `methodology_version_sha`, `embedding_model_id` and `embedding_model_version_sha` (frozen — never mix versions), `residualization_method`, `date_computed`, `parse_method` and `parse_quality_score`. The three normalization views (`drift_score_raw`, `drift_score_yearly_z`, `drift_score_sector_size_pctile`) are not redundant — each answers a different question (absolute, time-relative, cohort-relative) — and storing all three avoids re-computation and prevents downstream misuse.

## Conclusion

The defensible Layer 1 design starts from the empirical floor that a disciplined bag-of-words pipeline (TF-IDF cosine, MinHash Jaccard, LM dictionary deltas, log-file-size readability) reproduces nearly all of what the Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen literature actually demonstrated, while dense embeddings on this specific task have limited evidence and substantial liabilities (model-version drift, opacity, GPU cost, no native direction, FinMTEB-documented underperformance on financial STS). Embeddings earn their place as paraphrase-robustness guardrails on top of lexical primaries, not as replacements. The interpretable LM-feature track is non-negotiable because it is the only track that produces a *direction* of drift and a human-readable *what changed* — both required for downstream layer integration and for any regulator-defensible production deployment.

The four highest-value internal experiments before signal goes live: (1) **out-of-sample replication of Cohen-Malloy-Nguyen on 2014–2024 filings** using the original methodology, against a target of 3–8% annualized FF5 alpha gross of frictions; (2) **head-to-head benchmark of TF-IDF cosine vs Fin-E5 vs FinTextSim vs Liu-Yang-Tam on FinSTS and our own held-out drift-prediction task** to settle whether finance-fine-tuned dense embeddings actually beat BoW for change detection; (3) **algorithmic-listening adaptation rate measurement** (Cao-Jiang-Yang-Zhang 2023 framework) on 2011/2018/2022 instrumental events to estimate signal decay; (4) **section-extraction accuracy audit** on 200 random accession numbers across a stratified small-/mid-/large-cap sample to set the floor for downstream signal accuracy.

The single most important empirical caveat is that **filing language drift is partially endogenous to its own publication**: Cao-Jiang-Yang-Zhang demonstrate firms strategically adjust to defeat NLP detection; the FinMTEB and Liu-Yang-Tam findings suggest that off-the-shelf sophistication is not a substitute for methodological discipline; and the absence of a peer-reviewed post-2014 replication of the headline anomaly means the most-cited number in the literature is an in-sample, pre-publication, section-specific point estimate. The honest stance is that the layer is built to *measure* drift cleanly and expose it cleanly to other layers — not to promise a 188 bps/month alpha.
